Notre Dame Teaching Experience

Introduction to American Politics
This course examined the U.S. political system from the point of view of democratic theory, asking whether and to what extent American politics meets conventional understandings of democracy. The course introduced the basic concepts and tools for studying political behavior and public opinion. I led 3 weekly discussion sections with an emphasis on the salience of race in American politics.
Introduction to World Politics
This course introduced and examined the emergence of nation-states and variation in political development and governance around the globe. The course further delved into the fall of Communism, threats to Liberal democracy, intractable conflicts, and the rise of populism. I led 3 weekly discussion sections with a strong emphasis on developing students' writing. I also guest lectured on societal responses to neoliberalism in Central America.
Elections and Social Protest in Latin America
This course examined the principle-agent problem and the role of horizontal and vertical accountability mechanisms in Latin America by providing an overview of democratization, economic reforms, and electoral and social dynamics in Mexico, Bolivia, Chile, Argentina, Venezuela, and Guatemala. I guest lectured on indigenous resistance to authorianism and criminal-rule in Mexico.
Dictatorship, Democracy, and Civil War
This course examined the causes and consequences of criminal wars and large-scale criminal violence and the efficacy of transitional justice processes, truth commissions, and security sector reforms. In the shift to online instruction during COVID-19, I co-instructed 4 weekly small group lectures on Mexico, Guatemala, and El Salvador. I further designed a policy-applied project that challenged students to apply the course content to explain the persistence of violence in Central America to U.S. asylum law adjudicators. I won an Excellence in Teaching award for this course.
The 2020 U.S. Presidential Election
This course examined the presidential election in real time providing historical context while analyzing contemporary political events from the invisible primary to the party conventions and from the presidential debates to the post-election period. I led 3 selected discussion sections on special topics including political polarization, electoral threats to democracy, and systemic racism.

Freedom Summer Collegiate Co-Instructor of Record

Race and Policing
In the last quarter of the twentieth century, the U.S. has experienced an increase in mass incarceration and detention. The U.S. remains politically divided on the issues of police violence, crime control, and immigration enforcement. Some have denounced penal populism as incompatible with democratic norms and infringing on civil and human rights, and called for re-investments in communities and reducing the carceral state. Still, others have doubled-down on punitive policies on the grounds of concerns for public safety and national sovereignty. Why does the U.S. lead the way in incarcerating and disenfranchising its citizens and restricting the rights of non-citizens? Why are some groups punished and surveilled disproportionately? Does the American public prefer punishment? How have impacted communities responded? Using readings from across disciplines, students will explore whether, and to what extent, race and policing in the U.S. challenge fundamental conceptions of democracy, federalism, and citizenship. I co-taught this course with Adrian King at Freedom Summer Collegiate for underrepresented students in the Missisippi delta.
